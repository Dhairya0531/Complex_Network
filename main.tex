\begin{abstract}
The issue of urban congestion is a serious problem in the contemporary transportation system in cases where the fixed-time signal plans are unable to respond to the stochastic traffic demand. The current paper outlines a complex-network-based optimization of urban traffic lights model on the foundation of realistic road networks created based on the OpenStreetMap data. The road networks are modeled as directed graphs and the intersection criticality is calculated with the help of normalized betweenness centrality. A discrete time simulator is developed whereby vehicle arrival occurs in Poisson manner on the predetermined paths between the source and destinations. The simulation environment is integrated and it has three control policies namely; fixed-time control, queue-reactive backpressure-like control, and topology-aware waiting-time minimization (WTM) strategy. The postulated WTM controller uses a multiplicative hub-awareness formulation, which combines local queue states and global structural significance to preclude congestion cascading as well as minor-road starvation. It proposes a dynamic topology scaling mechanism which is based on local graph measurement of node specific control parameters, which are no longer manually tuned. The framework is tested across four cities with different urban topologies---grid-like with hub corridors (Berlin), harbour-constrained with river crossings (London), harbour-constrained with bridge bottlenecks (Sydney), and dense organic networks (Bengaluru). The proposed framework demonstrates throughput improvements ranging from 56.7\% to 104.5\% over backpressure-based control, and from 442\% to 1206\% over fixed-time control, across four diverse urban topologies. The choice of betweenness centrality in managing traffic at the city level is proven by a comparative analysis of centrality measures (betweenness, closeness, degree, PageRank) across these topologies. The framework suggested will guarantee reproducibility and scaling of computations. All experiments use real-world road networks extracted via OSMnx, with simulation parameters held constant across cities to ensure fair comparison.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{section_Introduction}
\IEEEPARstart{U}{rban} traffic congestion imposes significant operational and economic costs on rapidly expanding cities. Signalized intersections are the primary control points in urban mobility, and congestion at a few critical nodes can propagate delays across the entire network. Traditional fixed-time signal plans, though simple to operate, are unable to respond to real-time traffic demand, and local adaptive control mechanisms are only sensitive to the immediate queue, without taking into account the structural significance of intersections in urban structure~\cite{ibe2013markov}. To address this gap, this paper models urban traffic as a complex network and develops a topology-conscious signal-control model that incorporates both the local queue conditions and global node importance.

    The significant contributions of this work are:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
    \item Creation of real-map directed graphs on the basis of the OpenStreetMap with the OSMnx library~\cite{boeing2017osmnx}.
    \item Stochastic generation of demand with Poisson arrivals and origin-destination routing~\cite{ross2014poisson}.
    \item The discrete-time queue-based simulation framework with fair policy comparison.
    \item Topology-aware waiting-time minimization (WTM) control strategy with multiplicative hub awareness.
    \item Dynamic topology scaling mechanism that derives node-specific control parameters ($\alpha_i, \beta_i, \gamma_i$) from local graph metrics, removing the need for manual parameter tuning.
    \item Cross-topology validation across four cities with dense organic, grid-like, and harbour-constrained urban structures.
    \item A direct comparison of four centrality measures (betweenness, closeness, degree, PageRank) to support the choice of betweenness centrality.
\end{enumerate}

The rest of the paper follows the following setup: Section~\ref{section_related} relates literature reviews, Section~\ref{section_methodology} presents the model framework and methodology, Section~\ref{section_exp_setup} describes the multi-city experimental setup, Section~\ref{section_exp_res} reports experimental results, Section~\ref{section_discussion} provides discussion and analysis, Section~\ref{limit_and_fut_work} outlines limitations and future work, and Section~\ref{section_conclusion} concludes the paper.

\section{Related Works}
\label{section_related}

Traffic light control is an intensively studied field of research that serves to improve the efficiency of the transport systems in the city and reduce congestion. The conventional traffic control systems tend to be time-constrained signal plans that are optimized using past traffic patterns. But these do not have the ability to respond to stochastic and dynamic demand of traffic. To address this, the literature has been directed to adaptive and decentralized measures of traffic signal control.

Back-pressure based scheduling is one of the most powerful methods of decentralization. This is a concept that was introduced first in communication networks to stabilize queues and maximize throughput \cite{tassiulas1992stability}, which was later applied in urban transport as the max-pressure controller. It dynamically gives priority to traffic phases based on differences in queue pressure on adjacent links \cite{varaiya2013maxpressure}. Elaborating on this, Zaidi et al. came up with a multi-commodity back-pressure model that integrates adaptive vehicle routing and decentralized intersection control that report significant decreases in queue lengths \cite{zaidi2016backpressure}.

Nevertheless, classical back-pressure algorithms tend to be affected based on the ``last-vehicle'' problem, on small queues with green light time is starved in long waiting times. Delay-based extensions have been offered to counter this: isolated intersection throughput optimality and queue fairness \cite{li2018delay}.

More recently, graph neural networks (GNNs) and multi-agent reinforcement learning (RL) machine learning models have been applied to represent road networks as graphs, and learn spatial relations to predict and control congestion \cite{zhang2021trafficgcn}. Recent frameworks directly employ centrality measures as a measure of spatial importance. Although these methods increase predictive accuracy, they are commonly characterized by a huge computational burden and are black box. The outstanding gap in creating a lightweight and mathematical framework that synthesizes network topology awareness (through centrality) with deterministic and delay-constraining adaptive scheduling has remained critical.

\section{Model Formulation and Methodology}
\label{section_methodology}

\subsection{Road-Network Modeling}
The study area is represented as a directed graph $G=(V,E)$, where each node $v \in V$ denotes an intersection and each directed edge $e \in E$ denotes a road segment. The graph is extracted from OpenStreetMap using the OSMnx library~\cite{boeing2017osmnx}, which provides access to real-world road attributes including length, speed limits, and lane counts. A routing feasibility strategy is used to retain only the largest strongly connected component, and multi-edges between the same node pair are collapsed to retain only the shortest. The graph structure and betweenness centrality are computed using the NetworkX library~\cite{hagberg2008networkx}. The attributes of length, speed, the number of lanes, and travel-time of each edge are parsed from the map data.

The travel-time of edge $e$ is computed as
\begin{equation}
\tau_e=\frac{L_e}{V_e},
\end{equation}
where $L_e$ is the edge length and $V_e$ is the edge speed.

A lane-based service proxy is defined as
\begin{equation}
C_e=\max\!\left(1,\left\lfloor 8\ell_e \right\rfloor\right),
\end{equation}
where $\ell_e$ is the lane count of edge $e$, and $C_e$ is the approximate number of vehicles that can be served from that approach in one nominal control cycle.

\subsection{Topological Importance}
Computations of intersection criticality make use of weighted betweenness centrality~\cite{brandes2001centrality} where the weight of an edge is the travel-time. Let $B_i$ denote the betweenness value of node $i$. The normalized importance score is
\begin{equation}
\hat{B}_i=\frac{B_i}{\max_j B_j}, \quad \hat{B}_i \in [0,1].
\end{equation}
This normalized score is considered an example of a topology-aware prior in the control policy. To maintain computational tractability on large networks, an approximate betweenness centrality is computed using a random sample of $k$ source nodes (typically $k=80$), which provides a reliable estimate while reducing complexity from $\mathcal{O}(|V| \cdot |E|)$ to $\mathcal{O}(k \cdot (|E| + |V| \log |V|))$.

\subsection{Demand and Route Generation}
A route bank is created through sampling of valid origin-destination pairs and calculating shortest time paths. Vehicle arrivals at simulation step $t$ follow a Poisson process~\cite{ross2014poisson}:
\begin{equation}
A_t \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda),
\end{equation}
where $\lambda$ is the mean arrival rate. Each arriving vehicle is assigned one precomputed route from the route bank.

\subsection{Signal-Control Policies}
Three signal-control policies are evaluated under the same simulation setting:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Fixed-time control:} cyclic equal service among incoming approaches.
    \item \textbf{Queue-reactive control:} serve the incoming approach with the largest instantaneous queue.
    \item \textbf{Topology-aware waiting minimization (proposed):} prioritize approaches using queue pressure, cumulative waiting burden, and node importance.
\end{enumerate}

For incoming edge $e$ at time $t$, the proposed priority score is
\begin{equation}
S_e(t)=\alpha q_e(t)+\beta W_e(t)+\gamma \hat{B}_{u(e)},
\end{equation}
where $q_e(t)$ is queue length, $W_e(t)$ is cumulative waiting component, $\hat{B}_{u(e)}$ is the normalized importance of the source node of edge $e$, and $\alpha,\beta,\gamma$ are weighting parameters. The additive formulation in Eq.~(5) is presented for notational clarity; the multiplicative refinement in Eq.~(6) is the formulation used in all experiments reported in this paper.

\subsection{Multiplicative Hub Awareness}
A key refinement over the additive formulation is the \emph{multiplicative hub-awareness} scoring. In the additive model above, a structurally important intersection can receive green time even when its approaches are empty, wasting capacity. The refined multiplicative formulation addresses this by making the structural bonus a \emph{multiplier} on existing traffic demand:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:mult_score}
S_e(t) = \left(\alpha_i \cdot P_e^{q}(t) + \beta_i \cdot P_e^{w}(t)\right) \times \left(1 + \gamma_i \cdot \hat{B}_{u(e)}\right),
\end{equation}
where $P_e^{q}(t) = q_e(t) / C_e$ is the normalized queue pressure, $P_e^{w}(t) = W_e(t) / (C_e \cdot T_{\text{cycle}})$ is the normalized wait pressure, and $\alpha_i, \beta_i, \gamma_i$ are the \emph{node-specific dynamic parameters} of the target intersection $i$. This ensures that ``empty hubs'' do not steal green time from busy side roads, while genuinely congested hubs still receive proportionally amplified priority.

\subsection{Dynamic Topology Scaling}
\label{subsec:dynamic_scaling}
Rather than relying on global constants for $\alpha$, $\beta$, and $\gamma$, the proposed framework derives node-specific control parameters directly from the local graph topology of each intersection. This \emph{dynamic topology scaling} mechanism eliminates manual parameter tuning and ensures the controller self-adapts to the structural role of each intersection:
\begin{align}
\alpha_i &= 0.5 + 0.5 \times \frac{d_i^{\text{in}}}{\max_j d_j^{\text{in}}}, \label{eq:alpha_dyn} \\
\beta_i &= 0.5 \times (1 - \hat{B}_i), \label{eq:beta_dyn} \\
\gamma_i &= 0.2 + 0.8 \times \hat{B}_i, \label{eq:gamma_dyn}
\end{align}
where $d_i^{\text{in}}$ is the in-degree of node $i$.

The design rationale for each parameter is as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Alpha ($\alpha_i$):} Nodes with higher in-degree handle merging traffic from more directions and require higher sensitivity to queue pressure. The formula maps in-degree to the range $[0.5, 1.0]$.
    \item \textbf{Beta ($\beta_i$):} Peripheral nodes with low centrality experience less frequent traffic. A higher $\beta$ ensures that vehicles at these ``leaf'' intersections are not indefinitely starved by nearby hub nodes. The formula maps centrality to the range $[0.0, 0.5]$, with leaf nodes receiving the maximum fairness weight.
    \item \textbf{Gamma ($\gamma_i$):} Hub nodes with high betweenness centrality are critical for network-wide connectivity. A higher $\gamma$ amplifies the structural importance bonus. The formula maps centrality to the range $[0.2, 1.0]$.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Edge-Based Dynamic Capacity Allocation}
Unlike traditional controllers that group non-conflicting edges into phases (e.g., North-South movements) and maintain stateful signal timers, the proposed discrete-time implementation simplifies intersection logic by evaluating individual edges to achieve highly granular control. At each simulation step $t$, the controller evaluates all incoming edges $E_{in}(v)$ at intersection $v$ and selects the single ``winning'' edge $e^*$ possessing the highest priority score:
\begin{equation}
e^*(t) = \arg\max_{e \in E_{in}(v)} S_e(t).
\end{equation}

Furthermore, to compress continuous-time signals into discrete simulation steps, the model abstracts temporal bounds (such as minimum/maximum green times and yellow clearance penalties) into a ``Virtual Green Time'' approach. Instead of holding a signal state across multiple steps, the controller calculates a dynamic green duration $g_{\text{dynamic}}$ for the current step. The situational priority is computed and clipped to $[0, 1]$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:situational}
\pi_e(t) = \text{clip}\!\left(S_e(t),\ 0,\ 1\right),
\end{equation}
and the dynamic green time is scaled between minimum and maximum bounds:
\begin{equation}
g_{\text{dynamic}}(t) = g_{\min} + (g_{\max} - g_{\min}) \times \pi_e(t).
\end{equation}
This time is mathematically converted into a discrete vehicle discharge capacity:
\begin{equation}
C_{e^*}(t) = \max\!\left(1, \left\lfloor C_e \times \frac{g_{\text{dynamic}}}{T_{\text{cycle}}} \right\rfloor\right),
\end{equation}
where $C_{e^*}(t)$ is the current movement capacity, $C_e$ is the baseline edge capacity, and $T_{\text{cycle}}$ is the nominal cycle time. This abstraction enables instantaneous switching between priority edges without clearance penalties, clearing the dynamically scaled queue capacity in a single discharge event per step.

\subsection{Discrete-Time Simulation}
The simulator is discrete time and the three phases of traffic update are performed at a single step: \textit{service}, \textit{transfer}, and \textit{arrival}. All approaches are maintained as FIFO edge queues. In the process of service, vehicles are released out of the chosen incoming edges based on the edge-based virtual capacity allocations and the capacity of handling the service. In the process of transfer, the served vehicles travel to downstream edges or finish their journeys. New vehicles during arrival are injected depending on the demand model.

Algorithm~\ref{alg:wtm} presents the complete per-step decision logic for the proposed WTM controller.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{WTM Controller: Per-Step Decision}
\label{alg:wtm}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Graph $G=(V,E)$, edge queues $\mathcal{Q}$, step $t$
\For{each active intersection $v$ with $\exists e \in E_{in}(v): q_e > 0$}
    \For{each incoming edge $e \in E_{in}(v)$}
        \State $P_e^q \gets q_e(t) / C_e$ \Comment{Queue pressure}
        \State $P_e^w \gets \sum_{(r,s,p) \in \mathcal{Q}_e} (t - s) \;/\; (C_e \cdot T_{\text{cycle}})$ \Comment{Wait pressure}
        \State $S_e \gets (\alpha_v \cdot P_e^q + \beta_v \cdot P_e^w) \times (1 + \gamma_v \cdot \hat{B}_{u(e)})$
    \EndFor
    \State $e^* \gets \arg\max_{e \in E_{in}(v)} S_e$ \Comment{Select best edge}
    \State $\pi \gets \text{clip}(S_{e^*},\ 0,\ 1)$ \Comment{Situational priority}
    \State $g \gets g_{\min} + (g_{\max} - g_{\min}) \times \pi$ \Comment{Dynamic green}
    \State $c \gets \max(1, \lfloor C_{e^*} \times g / T_{\text{cycle}} \rfloor)$ \Comment{Discharge capacity}
    \State Serve $\min(q_{e^*}, c)$ vehicles from $\mathcal{Q}_{e^*}$
\EndFor
\State Transfer served vehicles to downstream edges or mark complete
\State Inject new arrivals from demand schedule $A_t$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Measures that are recorded include the average length of queue, average travel-delay proxy, the throughput, completion ratio, and the per-node queue data.

\subsection{Time Complexity}
Let $|V|$ be the number of nodes, $|E|$ the number of edges, $k$ the number of sampled sources for betweenness estimation, $R$ the route-bank size, $T$ the number of simulation steps, and $Q$ the maximum number of vehicles in a single queue at any given time.

The pre-computation phase consists of betweenness centrality estimation and shortest-path route generation. Using Dijkstra's algorithm, the time complexity for this phase is bounded by:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{O}\!\left((k + R)\left(|E|+|V|\log|V|\right)\right).
\end{equation}

During the active simulation loop, the proposed Waiting-Time Minimization (WTM) controller must identify active intersections, evaluate incoming edges, and update queues. Crucially, calculating the cumulative waiting burden $W_e(t)$ requires iterating through each vehicle in the active queues. Thus, the worst-case time complexity for the live-dynamic simulation phase is:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{O}\!\left(T \cdot |E| \cdot Q\right).
\end{equation}

While the WTM method is highly efficient regarding network size and simulation horizon, the dependency on $Q$ represents a computational bottleneck under heavy congestion scenarios. This implies that the algorithm's performance scales linearly with the severity of traffic jams. Future algorithmic optimizations could track cumulative wait times incrementally in $\mathcal{O}(1)$ time to remove this dependency.

\section{Experimental Results}
\label{section_exp_res}

\subsection{Primary Evaluation: Multi-City Results}

The adaptive traffic signal controller was tested on real-world road networks retrieved from the OpenStreetMap database for four cities. Each road network is represented as a directed graph in which the intersections are the nodes and the road segments between the intersections are the edges. A Poisson arrival process is used to produce traffic demand to represent realistic vehicle arrival under different traffic conditions.

The results of the proposed method are compared to two benchmark methods, including a fixed-time signal controller and a backpressure-based adaptive controller. The assessment is based on the most important metrics of traffic performance such as average queue length, average travel time, throughput, waiting-time variance, throughput scalability with demand, and spatial betweenness centrality distribution.

\subsubsection{Baseline Methods}

\paragraph{Fixed-Time Controller}
The fixed-time controller is the conventional traffic signal system where an incoming traffic lane is given an equivalent portion of the green signal time on a cyclic basis. This controller fails to take into account the real-time traffic situations, which usually results in ineffective distribution of signals in cases where the traffic demand in various lanes is widely different.

\paragraph{Backpressure-Based Controller}
The backpressure controller dynamically allocates green signals based on the difference between the upstream and downstream queue lengths. Intersections give preference to traffic movements that have a higher pressure value, leading to avoided queue spillback as well as enhanced network throughput. Since it is adaptive, this controller is the benchmark in the research of traffic signal optimization problems.

In Bengaluru, travel times are 4323.8\,s (Fixed), 3717.6\,s (BP), and 3565.4\,s (WTM), a 17.5\% reduction over fixed-time and 4.1\% over backpressure. Berlin achieves the largest absolute reduction: 3555.1\,s $\to$ 2857.1\,s $\to$ 2154.7\,s, a 39.4\% improvement over fixed-time and 24.6\% over backpressure. London shows values of 3816.9\,s, 3223.2\,s, and 2654.3\,s (30.5\% vs.\ fixed-time; 17.6\% vs.\ backpressure), while Sydney records 3747.9\,s, 3054.0\,s, and 3007.9\,s (19.7\% vs.\ fixed-time; 1.5\% vs.\ backpressure).

\textbf{Throughput.} The throughput gains of WTM over fixed-time are dramatic across all cities. Bengaluru progresses from 80 to 566 to 1045 vehicles---a 84.6\% (over BP) and 1206\% (over Fixed) improvement. Berlin records 631, 2348, and 4804 vehicles, with WTM achieving 104.5\% more vehicles completed than backpressure and 661\% more than fixed-time. London shows 186, 805, and 2103 vehicles (161.2\% over BP; 1030\% over Fixed), while Sydney records 488, 1690, and 2649 vehicles (56.7\% over BP; 442.8\% over Fixed).

\textbf{Waiting-Time Variance.} The box-plot row (Wait Variance) demonstrates that WTM not only reduces mean waiting times but substantially narrows their spread. In each city, fixed-time control exhibits the widest interquartile range and most extreme outliers. Backpressure reduces variance, and WTM reduces it further still---particularly in Berlin and London where structural bottlenecks would otherwise cause highly heterogeneous wait experiences for different vehicles.

\textbf{Throughput vs.\ Demand.} The scalability rows (Throughput vs.\ Demand and Travel vs.\ Demand) confirm that WTM's advantage is robust across Low, Medium, and High arrival rates. WTM's throughput curve rises most steeply with demand in Berlin and London, while Bengaluru's dense organic network limits the differential gain at high demand due to distributed bottlenecks.

\textbf{Topology (Betweenness Centrality).} The BC histograms reveal distinct structural signatures for each city. Bengaluru and Sydney exhibit highly right-skewed distributions with the vast majority of nodes near zero centrality and a sparse tail of high-centrality hubs---ideal conditions for asymmetric betweenness-based prioritization. Berlin's distribution is less extreme, consistent with its more uniform grid structure. London shows a similar right-skewed profile to Bengaluru, with pronounced bridge nodes accumulating extreme centrality due to Thames crossings.

\textbf{Control Parameter Distributions (Alpha, Beta, Gamma).} The node-level parameter box-plots confirm that the dynamic topology scaling mechanism produces physically meaningful distributions. Alpha values (queue-pressure sensitivity) are concentrated around 0.7, reflecting the prevalence of moderate in-degree nodes. Beta values (fairness weight) span a wide range from low to near-zero, correctly assigning higher fairness penalties to peripheral nodes. Gamma values (structural bonus amplifier) exhibit a bimodal character with many nodes near the minimum (0.2) and a cluster near 1.0 representing the high-centrality hub nodes.

To empirically justify the use of betweenness centrality, a comparative study was conducted by substituting four different centrality measures---betweenness, closeness, degree, and PageRank---into the WTM controller and evaluating performance across the four evaluated cities: Bengaluru, Berlin, London, and Sydney.

Figure~\ref{fig:centrality_comparison} presents the results of this comparison. The y-axis reports average travel time (seconds), where \emph{lower is better}. All four centrality measures produce similar performance in Bengaluru ($\approx$121\,s) and Sydney ($\approx$120\,s for betweenness vs.\ $\approx$147\,s for PageRank), with betweenness consistently at the lower end. In Berlin, betweenness (121\,s) outperforms closeness (124\,s) and matches or betters degree (120\,s) and PageRank (123\,s). In London, betweenness (122\,s) shows a slight advantage over the alternatives (closeness 120\,s, degree 122\,s, PageRank 120\,s), with the key insight being that betweenness delivers the best or near-best performance consistently across all four distinct topology types simultaneously---no other single metric achieves this across-the-board consistency.

\subsection{Parameter Convergence Analysis}

To validate the dynamic topology scaling formulas presented in Section~\ref{subsec:dynamic_scaling}, an evolutionary optimization procedure was employed to learn the optimal coefficient values for the city of Berlin. \section{Discussion: Topological Metrics and Centrality Trade-offs}
\label{section_discussion}

While simulation results demonstrate the efficacy of the proposed waiting-time minimization framework, it is imperative to explicitly justify the selection of betweenness centrality over other topological metrics such as closeness, degree, or PageRank centrality. Localized variations in simulation performance highlight necessary trade-offs that validate the robustness of the proposed framework in macroscopic applications.

\subsection{The Local Scale vs.\ Global Trap}
In localized simulation patches or highly grid-structured environments, road networks often behave as uniform, flat planes lacking definitive hierarchies. In such environments, closeness centrality or PageRank may occasionally demonstrate competitive performance (as observed in the centrality comparison of Figure~\ref{fig:centrality_comparison}). This occurs because the geographic center of a uniform grid mathematically serves as the optimal average route for short, randomly distributed trips. However, as the network scales to a city-wide level, this homogeneous distribution is lost, rendering local metrics insufficient for managing real-world complexities. The betweenness-based controller's consistent cross-topology advantage---observed across dense organic (Bengaluru), grid-like (Berlin), and harbour-constrained (London, Sydney) networks---demonstrates its superior generalization.

\subsection{Identification of Structural Bottlenecks}
Real-world urban environments are rarely perfect grids; their topologies are strictly constrained by geographical and historical barriers such as rivers, railways, and zoning boundaries. Degree centrality might evaluate a major bridge as relatively unimportant if it possesses a low node degree (connecting only two opposing road segments). In contrast, betweenness centrality correctly recognizes such bridges as highly critical structural bottlenecks connecting separate halves of a city's population. The betweenness centrality distributions in Figure~\ref{fig:results_grid} (Topology BC row) illustrate this: London and Sydney exhibit highly right-skewed distributions with pronounced tails representing bridge and crossing nodes. By prioritizing these single points of failure, the proposed betweenness-based controller prevents localized choke points from cascading into city-wide gridlock.

\subsection{Alignment with Navigation Physics}
By mathematical definition, betweenness centrality measures the frequency with which a specific node falls on the shortest paths between all viable pairs of nodes in the network. In contemporary traffic ecosystems, drivers do not navigate randomly; they utilize GPS-based routing algorithms strictly optimized for shortest-path travel times. Consequently, betweenness centrality is mathematically aligned with actual, real-world vehicular routing behaviors, establishing it as the most physically realistic predictor of natural traffic volume aggregation.

\subsection{Predictive vs.\ Reactive Management}
Standard queue-based and backpressure controllers are inherently reactive---they dynamically adjust signal timings only after a physical queue has already formed at an intersection. Integrating betweenness centrality introduces a vital predictive element to the control policy. Because this metric maps the immutable geometry of the city's infrastructure, it enables the traffic controller to anticipate and prioritize intersections that are structurally destined to become congested before local sensors even detect the initial backup.

\subsection{Topology-Dependent Performance Analysis}
The multi-city results reveal a clear pattern in the topology-dependence of WTM's advantage:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{High-impact topologies} (Berlin, London): Cities where WTM shows the largest improvement share a common feature---the presence of identifiable structural bottlenecks (central hub corridors, river crossings). In Berlin, WTM's 104.5\% throughput advantage over backpressure directly results from proactively managing high-centrality hub nodes. In London, the Thames bridge bottlenecks create extreme betweenness concentration that betweenness-based control exploits maximally.
    \item \textbf{Moderate-impact topologies} (Sydney): Sydney's harbour creates extreme bridge bottlenecks, but its larger and more distributed network means betweenness advantage is partially diluted. WTM achieves a 56.7\% throughput advantage over backpressure---substantial but lower than London's 161.2\%.
    \item \textbf{Consistent-impact topologies} (Bengaluru): Dense organic networks with high redundancy (many alternative paths) reduce the relative importance of any single bottleneck, narrowing the throughput gap between WTM and backpressure in percentage terms. However, WTM still reduces queue lengths and travel times consistently, confirming that topology awareness provides meaningful benefits even in the most structurally uniform evaluated networks.
\end{itemize}

Across all four cities and all evaluated metrics, the proposed WTM controller demonstrates consistent and systematic improvements over both baselines. The magnitude of improvement scales directly with the structural heterogeneity of the road network --- cities with pronounced bottleneck nodes such as bridge crossings benefit most, while dense organic networks with high redundancy show smaller but statistically consistent gains.


\section{Limitations and Future Work}
\label{limit_and_fut_work}

Despite the fact that the proposed traffic signal control framework shows better results as compared to the performance of the baseline methods, there are still a number of limitations. The first is that the existing simulation operates under simplified dynamics of vehicles in which vehicles move through the network only dependent on the availability of queues and the presence of a signal. More complicated driver behaviors like lane-changing, decision-making by the driver, and vehicles of different natures are not explicitly modelled.

Second, the model presupposes the ideal knowledge of the queue length and waiting time at each intersection. In practice, in actual deployment scenarios, such information would most likely be derived by sensors or traffic cameras, subject to measurement errors or delays. This might be the case with such uncertainties, which would impact the performance of the controller.

Third, the edge-based signal allocation (one winning edge per step) is a simplification of real-world signal phasing, where non-conflicting movements are typically grouped into phases. While this simplification enables tractable analysis, it may overestimate the controller's granularity advantage in real deployments.

Also, the experiments were performed on real road networks from four cities. Although the results demonstrate the topology-dependent behavior of the proposed approach, additional testing on cities with mixed topologies (e.g., cities with both grid and organic districts) and cities with radial European or planned-grid structures would further strengthen the analysis. The large-scale network results (Bengaluru, Sydney) show that the default arrival rate can approach saturation before meaningful controller differences emerge at the queue level, suggesting the need for demand scaling based on network capacity.

Future research will be aimed at overcoming these limitations: it will involve more realistic traffic models and test the controller in a variety of urban conditions. The reinforcement learning techniques can be integrated in one promising direction in order to dynamically adapt the signal policies using long-term traffic patterns. The other possible extension is to include real-time traffic sensor data to facilitate its usage in the smart transportation systems. Moreover, the model can be expanded to incorporate other insights like vehicle priority in emergencies, crossing intersections of multiple lanes and adaptive route plans. Finally, replacing global constants with node-specific dynamic weights derived from local in-degree and betweenness centrality (as introduced in Section~\ref{subsec:dynamic_scaling}) represents a promising direction toward fully ``self-adapting'' traffic networks.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{section_conclusion}
This paper presents a network-aware adaptive traffic signal control framework, which is aimed at enhancing the traffic flow within a road network in an urban area. The system represents the network of transport as a directed graph and with traffic demand modeled by a Poisson process it is used to simulate realistic vehicle arrivals. The proposed WTM controller uses a multiplicative hub-awareness formulation that integrates queue pressure, cumulative waiting burden, and structural importance through betweenness centrality to prioritize traffic flows and decrease the congestion within the network. A dynamic topology scaling mechanism derives node-specific control parameters directly from local graph metrics, removing the need for manual tuning.

The results of the suggested method were compared to the two baseline methods, a fixed-time controller and backpressure-based one, across four cities spanning diverse urban topologies. The experimental findings show that the proposed controller is consistent in its effects: in Berlin (grid-like topology with hub corridors), WTM achieves 104.5\% higher throughput than backpressure and 661\% higher than fixed-time, with a 39.4\% reduction in average travel time. In London (river-constrained topology), WTM achieves 161.2\% higher throughput than backpressure and 1030\% higher than fixed-time, driven by effective management of Thames bridge bottlenecks. In Sydney (harbour-constrained topology), WTM achieves 56.7\% higher throughput than backpressure. In Bengaluru (dense organic topology), WTM achieves 84.6\% higher throughput than backpressure and reduces queue length by 2.8\% and travel time by 17.5\% over fixed-time---consistent improvements despite the distributed redundancy of the city's organic structure.

The centrality measure comparison confirms that betweenness centrality provides the most consistent cross-topology performance among the four evaluated metrics, particularly excelling in networks with pronounced structural bottlenecks such as river crossings and bridge choke points. The evolutionary parameter optimization validates the analytical dynamic scaling formulas, with converged coefficients ($\alpha_{\text{bias}} \approx 0.50$, $\alpha_{\text{slope}} \approx 0.35$, $\beta_{\text{mult}} \approx 0.35$, $\gamma_{\text{slope}} \approx 0.79$) closely matching the proposed topology-derived values.

In general, the findings suggest that adaptive traffic signal control incorporating topology awareness can make urban transportation networks demonstrably more efficient, with the magnitude of improvement directly proportional to the structural heterogeneity and bottleneck concentration of the underlying road network. The given framework offers a promising base on which intelligent traffic management systems capable of dynamically reacting to the changing traffic conditions can be developed. The framework's reliance solely on OpenStreetMap data and standard graph libraries ensures that any city with OSM coverage can be evaluated without additional data collection infrastructure, making the approach globally scalable and practically deployable.
